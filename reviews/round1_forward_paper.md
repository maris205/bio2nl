Reviewer Comments:

Reviewer 1
The paper claims that language-trained LLMs can, without biological training, perform tasks like protein/DNA homology detection and simple function prediction. It argues that language and biological sequences share a common underlying structure, supported by scaling results, attention analysis, and a new BioPAWS benchmark.

Major concerns:
1. You claim “no biological training,” but Qwen/Gemini-class models almost certainly saw UniProt/SCOP/Swiss-Prot sequences and biology text during pretraining/RLHF. What is your decontamination evidence?

2. Why do ESM/ProtT5 drop to ~50% on remote homology under your setup? That contradicts a lot of practice where embedding + simple classifier works.

3. What could be the reason behind the precision and recall trend that is just opposite for Homologous and Non Homologous class, in Table 1?  I see your discussion right after the table that reflects the inherent difficulty in detecting homologous class, but why does it have that high precision then?

4. While framed as cross-domain discovery, the method effectively repurposes sequence models trained on natural language for pairwise classification tasks in proteins, raising concerns about whether the observed performance reflects true structural reasoning or general sequence similarity heuristics and potential pretraining leakage.

Minor Comments:
1. Labels in Fig 3 are barely readable. I revommend to make them bigger.

Reviewer 2
In this manuscript, the author introduces BioPAWS, a benchmark designed to test whether general-purpose language models can discriminate homologous and non-homologous biological sequences without domain-specific biological pretraining. The results suggest that GPT-2 fine-tuned only on English paraphrase detection can achieve high zero-shot accuracy on protein homology classification, and that larger instruction-tuned models further improve performance on standard and remote homology tasks. While the manuscript is appealing, many points require further clarification. Overall, the manuscript makes very strong claims that are not appropriately evaluated and lack controls to determine whether the observed patterns are due to the language models themselves or to biases in the protein sequences evaluated. Additionally, the manuscript lacks precise information about the methods employed, forcing readers to navigate the Github repository to understand crucial methodological aspects. Therefore, the manuscript should include this information, at least as supplementary material if space in the main text is limited. Regarding this last point, the accuracy results reported in Figure 2 seem to be based on a “prediction flipping” procedure, according to the repository, based on the test set. It is unclear why this procedure was used and how it affects the interpretation of the results.
1, The sentence “Crucially, none of these models were specifically trained on biological data prior to our experimental phase” makes a strong claim that is not sufficiently supported. Could the manuscript provide references or more concrete evidence showing that these models were not trained on biological data? Since several of these models were developed by third-party providers, there is no guarantee that the available training descriptions fully capture the actual training data used. Therefore, the manuscript should either substantiate this claim more carefully or rephrase it in a more cautious way.
2, Although netbooks have been released describing the construction of the homologous and non-homologous protein sequence datasets, the final datasets used for the evaluation have not been released and should be made available.
2.1, The manuscript should also provide a description of how the prediction of homologous/non-homologous pairs was conducted. This description should also include a comparative analysis of sequence length between homologous versus non-homologous, as well as the distribution of sequence similarity, obtained by classical methods, between groups and within groups. Moreover, it would be highly informative to report a hierarchical clustering of these sequences highlighting the label of each sequence (hom,non-hom)
3, In Figure 2a, is the distribution of score only covering from 0.5 to 0.85? Or did the panel cropped scores below 0.5 and above 0.85? In any case, this panel should show the distributions over the full length of the x axis (from 0 to 1).
3.1, It is unclear why the other two models (Llama and Qwen) were not included in the experiment presented in Figure 2. Their inclusion would help understand whether the observed pattern is consistent across models or whether it is GPT-2 dependent.
3.2, To confirm the effect of fine tuning, the manuscript should include controls. In addition to ESM-2 and ProtT5, an additional alternative is to include GPT-2 in which fine tune uses a shuffled PAWS-X to break sentence pairing.
3.2.1, Additionally, it would be beneficial to include a control raw GPT-2 in which its weights are randomly assigned so that its overall distribution mimics that observed in the original raw GPT-2.
3.2.1.1, To understand whether the homology detection is due to the classification head or the fine-tuned LLM itself, the manuscript should also report a control in which the weights of the classification head of the fine-tuned GPT-2 are replaced by those from a randomly initialization.
3.3, The confusion matrix should show the average across the multiple random seeds to make it consistent with Figure 2a, while the best case (actual Figure2b) should be moved to supplementary.
3.3.1, The manuscript should report how many seeds were used for the experiment.
3.4, It is unclear whether the accuracy reported in Figure 2 corresponds to raw predictions or to post hoc label-flipped predictions. In the released code, predictions are ‘flipped’ when raw accuracy is below 0.5 based on the test set (inside plot_and_save_confusion_matrix
 in https://github.com/maris205/biopaws/blob/main/2-gpt_ft_test_explain/1-gpt2_ft_en_test_protein_confusion.ipynb). This procedure should be explicitly reported in the manuscript as it is not aligned well with a typical zero-shot prediction as well as the rationale for using it. Additionally, the manuscript should mention if this procedure was also used in Table 2.
4, Figure 3a should include all the controls indicated in previous comments, as well as the other LLMs evaluated in the manuscript
5, The claim of detection of mutated amino acid residues in homologous sequences (Figure 3b) should be evaluated more systematically, particularly in sequences that conserve homology but have independently evolved during long time
6, Figure 4 requires control. How are protein positives and protein negatives projected when using just their amino acid sequences for t-SNE dimensionality reduction? This result should be included in the manuscript to demonstrate that the separation observed in Figure 4 is not purely driven by sequence composition biases between group pairs.
7, Table 2 shows Gemini-3, but this method was not included in Figure 1.
8, The claim “this +15-25% robustness gap (Table 2) demonstrates that general intelligence confers an advantage in abductive reasoning: when explicit sequence patterns vanish, LLMs leverage a "universal structural grammar" to infer conservation based on latent logic rather than memorized motifs.” should be toned down as it seems that at least Qwen-3 was trained with biological data as its CoT outputs show understanding about protein structural motifs. If this model was trained with biological data, it is not possible to discriminate if the observed gap is due to general intelligence or memorization.
